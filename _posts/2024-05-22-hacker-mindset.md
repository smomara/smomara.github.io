title: "The Hacker Mindset: Passion Over Profit"
---

Too many people today study computer science because they see it as a ticket to a high-paying job. They think a CS degree alone will guarantee them a cushy six-figure gig at a big-tech company. But this mercenary mindset is all wrong. You can't just go through the motions, learn a few data structures, solve a few leetcode problems, and expect to be showered with job offers.

The truth is, if you're studying CS primarily because you want to get rich, you're going to have a tough time competing with the real hackers - the ones who are in it for the love of the craft. As Paul Graham wrote in his essay [*Great Hackers*](https://paulgraham.com/gh.html), "Great hackers think of it as something they do for fun, and which they're delighted to find people will pay them for." It's this intrinsic passion and curiosity that makes them so good. They're relentless in developing their skills, not because it might lead to a bigger paycheck, but because they genuinely love solving hard problems and building cool things.

The world of tech is changing fast. With the rise of AI and more powerful tools, a small number of truly excellent hackers can now have an outsized impact. As artificial intelligence advances, it will increasingly automate routine coding tasks. The programmers who will thrive in this new world are those who can think creatively, solve novel problems, and build innovative systems. In other words, the passionate hackers - not the mercenary 9-to-5 code monkeys just doing it for the money.

Companies increasingly want the best of the best - the passionate, self-motivated people who live and breathe their craft. Skill and dedication are what matter now, not credentials. Those motivated solely by money will be left in the dust.

But if you love the craft - if you have that burning desire to master your tools, solve deep problems, and build the future - then you'll find your passion is worth far more than a mere paycheck. Nurture that hacker spirit, and you can't go wrong.
